## The Problem with Thought Experiments

"A runaway trolley is heading toward five people. You can pull a lever to divert it, but one person will die instead. Do you pull the lever?"

This is the trolley problem‚Äîphilosophy's most famous moral dilemma. But here's what's broken: **your brain doesn't engage with "five people."** It processes numbers, not humans.

The abstraction is the point for philosophers. They want to isolate ethical calculus: utilitarianism (save five, kill one) versus deontology (killing is wrong, even to save others).

But for anyone trying to actually *feel* the weight of moral decisions, abstraction is the failure.

## Three Innovations

### 1. Hamlet Mode (Thick vs. Thin Descriptions)

**Napkin Mode** (traditional philosophy):
- "5 people vs 1 person"
- Clean abstractions
- Easy calculus

**Hamlet Mode** (this project):
- Multi-paragraph backstories
- Sensory details (coal smoke, lye soap, Earl Grey tea)
- Personal debts (coffin unpaid, eggs owed, forms denied)
- Specific objects (letter unread, stone ungiven, 48 eggs in cart)

**The difference:** Specificity creates weight.

### 2. LEGOS Framework (Structured Stakes)

**L**ocation, **E**ntity, **G**oal, **O**bstacle, **S**hift, **S**olution

Traditional narrative hides stakes in prose. LEGOS makes them structurally visible:

**Gavril (Track A):**
```
Location: Main track, 40m from fork, hand on walking stick
Entity: Village blacksmith, 67, taught you to read, made mother's coffin
Goal: Sell horseshoes at market, read son's letter over tea
Obstacle: Limp (horse kick 1891) slows escape
Shift: If saved ‚Üí market continues | If killed ‚Üí letter never read
```

**Inspector Finch (Track B):**
```
Location: Spur track (unused 8 years), kneeling, writing ledger
Entity: Railway Authority District 7, denied your brake repair
Goal: Complete quarterly inspection, document vegetation
Obstacle: Form 17-B (DENIED), "Budget constraints," red ink
```

### 3. Hyperlegible Context Engineering

**Five principles:**

**1. Temporal Precision**
- Not "a trolley approaches" ‚Üí "2:17pm, the rust-red ore car thunders down"
- Not "recently" ‚Üí "six weeks ago"
- Not "soon" ‚Üí "seven seconds"

**2. Physical Reality**
- Not "you control a lever" ‚Üí "The lever is in your hand. Your grandfather polished it smooth over forty years."
- Not "the trolley" ‚Üí "40 tons of iron and ore. No brakes."

**3. Institutional Failure**
- Not "the brakes don't work" ‚Üí "Form 17-B: 'Emergency Brake Repair Request.' Status: DENIED. Reason: 'Insufficient evidence.' Signed: Inspector A. Finch."

**4. Personal Debts**
- Gavril: Made your mother's coffin (unpaid)
- Lena: Three weeks of eggs (6 rubles owed)
- Finch: Denied your repair request (caused this moment)

**5. Sensory Anchors**

## Design Wisdom

### Objects Carry Meaning

When someone dies, describe what happens to their objects:
- Gavril's letter: Pages torn, never read
- Lena's eggs: 48 shattered, yolk with blood
- Piotr's stone: Rolls into weeds, Anya never receives it
- Finch's Form 17-B: Lands face-up in weeds (irony made visible)

**Why:** Objects outlast people. They're evidence.

### Debts Create Stakes

Relationships = obligations = weight = reality

- You learned to read from Gavril
- You owe Lena 6 rubles
- You watched Piotr become man of house at twelve
- Finch denied your repair request (this is his fault too)

### Sensory Memory Persists

Long after you forget utilitarian calculus, you remember:
- The smell of coal smoke
- The weight of 6 rubles
- The sound echoing for seventeen seconds
- The lever, warm in your palm

## Context Engineering Insights

### What Makes Stakes Real?

**Not this:** "Five people will die"

**But this:**
- Specific age (67, limp from horse kick in 1891)
- Monetary debt (6 rubles for three weeks of eggs)
- Unfulfilled gift (stone for Anya, never given)
- Bureaucratic paper trail (Form 17-B DENIED, red ink)
- Temporal precision (2:17pm, six weeks ago, seventeen seconds)
- Physical objects (48 eggs, German fountain pen, walking stick)
- Sensory details (coal smoke, lye soap, Earl Grey tea)

## What This Teaches Us

### About Moral Philosophy

**Abstraction isn't clarity‚Äîit's avoidance.** The trolley problem's power comes from making you feel torn, not from clean ethical calculus. But you can't feel torn about "five people."

You can feel torn about:
- Gavril who taught you to read
- Lena whose children will starve
- Piotr with a stone for his sister
- Finch whose denial caused this

### About Context Engineering

**Structure reveals stakes.** When you force yourself to fill out LEGOS fields (Location, Entity, Goal, Obstacle, Shift, Solution), you can't hide behind vague language. Either the stakes are real and specific, or they're not.

### About Narrative Design

**Details aren't decoration‚Äîthey're load-bearing.** The letter unread, the stone ungiven, the eggs shattered, Form 17-B in the weeds. These aren't flavor text. They're what you carry after.

### About Memory

**We don't remember arguments. We remember:**
- Smells (coal smoke, lye soap)
- Sounds (seventeen seconds of echo)
- Objects (stone rolling away)
- Numbers (6 rubles, 48 eggs, 40 tons)
- Moments (the lever is cold now)
